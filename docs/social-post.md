# Nearfolk — social post draft

I'm building a game about the architecture of kindness.

Nearfolk is a cozy 90-second web game where you design a small cottage cluster around a shared commons, then watch your neighborhood come to life. It's my entry for the Cursor Vibe Jam 2026, a game jam with $35K in prizes where 90% of code has to be written by AI.

The idea came from my ACRE capstone. My partner Chandra Ellis and I spent six months designing Avenues West Cottages, a 15-home pocket neighborhood proposal for a site on W. State Street in Milwaukee. Pocket neighborhoods are a concept from architect Ross Chapin: small clusters of homes gathered around a shared commons, typically 8 to 16 households. His line that stuck with me: "Shared space is the heart of a pocket neighborhood. It's what holds it together and what gives it vitality."

Chapin's whole approach is about layers of personal space. A low fence. A small yard. A covered porch. Then your front door. Each layer gives you a choice about how much of the world you let in. And the way homes face each other matters. He talks about "nesting" cottages so the open side of one house faces the closed side of the next, so you get community without losing privacy. The direction your porch faces determines who you see, who you wave to, who becomes the neighbor you actually know.

That's the game. You place cottages on a grid and rotate them. Face two porches toward each other across the commons, and a connection forms. Block a sightline with a tree, and it doesn't. Leave someone with no connections at all, and you get a penalty and a sad little cloud over their head. You're not optimizing money or density. You're arranging space so that good things want to happen in it.

That's what I mean by "architecture of kindness." Chapin writes about pocket neighborhoods creating "shirt-tail aunties and uncles just beyond the front gate," the kind of informal care that happens when people can see each other from their porches. The design of a space can make people more likely to notice each other, check in, cross paths on the way home. In the game, that's the score.

The part I keep thinking about: a Day Director inspired by RimWorld's storyteller AI, but flipped. RimWorld's Cassandra picks disasters. Nearfolk's Director picks kindnesses. It looks at who's lonely, who hasn't met yet, who has the strongest connections, and writes a small story. "June noticed Maya hadn't been out all week. She brought coffee." Same engine. Opposite feeling.

Two days in. 800 lines of code. 135KB bundle. 8 piece types on a 10x10 isometric grid with golden-hour lighting and tilt-shift blur. The scoring engine already works. Place two cottages facing each other across open space, watch the score go up. Drop a tree between them, watch it go back down. That one interaction is the whole thesis.

Shipping May 1.
